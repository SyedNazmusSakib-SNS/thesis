The Hallucination Problem in Depth

Literature Review | Slide 13

Medical VLMs are prone to generating unsupported findings. In radiology, these hallucinations are not just linguistic errors but represent significant clinical safety risks .

Figure 3: Taxonomy of Hallucinations
Hallucination Type Definition Clinical Risk Severity
False Positive

Reporting a finding (e.g., nodule, effusion) that is not present in the image.

Unnecessary treatment, radiation exposure from follow-up CTs, patient anxiety.

High
Incorrect Localization

Correct finding attributed to the wrong anatomical region (e.g., Left vs Right).

Procedural errors (e.g., wrong-side thoracentesis), incorrect surgical planning.

High
Severity Distortion

Misrepresenting the extent or size of a finding (e.g., "Mild" vs "Severe" edema).

Misjudgment of disease course urgency; delayed escalation of care.

Medium
Device Hallucination

Fabricating medical devices (e.g., tubes, pacemakers) not present in the patient.

Confusion about patient status; assuming intubation when patient is breathing spontaneously.

Medium
Temporal Hallucination

Fabricating a change (e.g., "worsening pneumonia") when the condition is stable.

Critical longitudinal misinterpretation; changing therapy based on false progression.

Critical

Key Research Insight

Current metrics (BLEU/ROUGE) often fail to capture these errors. Our approach specifically targets Temporal Hallucinations via differential verification.
